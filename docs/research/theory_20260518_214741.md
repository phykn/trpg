# Theory Cycle 15

## Claim

`TransitionValidity` is independent from `FictionContinuity`.

## Evidence

They can fail separately:

- A teleport can be rule-valid while the narration forgets pursuit, aftermath, or prior risks.
- An NPC rescue can be beautifully foreshadowed but procedurally invalid if the table agreed no outside intervention was possible.
- Fast travel can be system-valid while fiction barely explains time, danger, or companions.
- A parser command can be legal while prior fiction contradicts it.

## Objection

In many freeform or LLM-mediated games, players only observe validity through narration.

If no explicit rule, procedure, visible affordance, table norm, genre contract, or agreed exception is exposed, then validity becomes hidden authority: "it is valid because the narrator accepted it."

That makes `TransitionValidity` hard to falsify and lets the narrator smooth over invalid transitions with plausible prose.

## Revision

Rename and sharpen the condition:

```text
PlayableContinuity =
ExposedTransitionValidity + FictionContinuity + AgencyContinuity
```

`ExposedTransitionValidity` means:

- the accepted transition is possible under rules, constraints, genre contract, table norm, or agreed exception;
- the relevant validity criterion is available to participants before or during acceptance.

A validity claim is testable only if a participant can name what would have made the transition invalid without merely saying "the fiction would not explain it."

## Why it is stronger

The revision keeps validity independent from fiction while preventing hidden narrator authority from masquerading as validity.

It predicts separate failure modes:

- a legal move can feel incoherent;
- a coherent scene can be procedurally illegitimate;
- a transition with no exposed validity criterion cannot be trusted as valid.
