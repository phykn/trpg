# Theory Cycle 17

## Claim

Explicit invalidity legibility is required for transitions that are risky, costly, contested, surprising, irreversible, or challenged.

## Evidence

- Opening a normal door does not need explanation.
- Refusing a lockpick attempt does need explanation if the door was expected to be locked and pickable.
- Fast travel being unavailable needs explanation when the player previously learned it as an affordance.
- "You can't go that way" is too thin when the room description implied an exit.
- Refusing to persuade a guard needs a visible reason category when the refusal blocks a meaningful plan.

## Objection

The list is directionally right but too broad and overlapping.

Risky, costly, contested, irreversible, and surprising mix causes, effects, and detection mechanisms. Almost any transition can be called surprising or costly from some perspective.

`Challenged` is different. It is not an intrinsic property of a transition. It is a procedural escalation.

## Revision

A transition is nontrivial when accepting or rejecting it would affect one of these:

1. `StakesChanging`: danger, resources, position, relationships, or reward.
2. `AuthorityCrossing`: rules, established constraints, another character's agency, or GM/system ownership.
3. `ContinuitySensitive`: the player's reasonable belief about fiction, affordances, or causal chain.
4. `CommitmentHeavy`: hard-to-reverse state, death, permanent loss, locked choices, or major narrative branching.

And:

5. `Challenged`: any transition becomes nontrivial if a relevant player challenge questions its validity.

## Why it is stronger

The revision asks what changes if the transition is accepted or rejected. That is more testable than asking whether it feels risky, costly, or surprising.

It also separates intrinsic nontriviality from procedural escalation.

## Next question

What minimum form must invalidity legibility take: rule, hidden fact category, available alternative, or path to making the transition valid?
